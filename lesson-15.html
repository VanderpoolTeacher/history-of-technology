Lesson 15: Researching a Technology: Methods and Sources

Module: 8 — Case-Study Capstone · Hours: 2 (120 min)

Introduction

You type the name of a technology into a search box — say, “the printing press” — and the screen floods with results: ten thousand of them, a museum page, a hobbyist’s blog, a scanned document from the 1490s, a listicle of “10 inventions that changed the world.” A flush of confidence runs through you. The answer is clearly in there somewhere. You open the first tab, then the fifth, then the twentieth.

An hour later you have forty tabs, a dozen contradictory dates, and a sinking feeling. One page swears the press appeared in 1440, another in 1450; one calls it revolutionary, another calls it a clever copy of older ideas. You cannot tell which to believe, you have written nothing, and the abundance that felt like a gift has thickened into fog. More finding has not brought you closer to knowing.

Here is the quiet truth this lesson is built on: a researcher is not someone who can find a lot, but someone who can narrow, judge, and record with discipline — and a project that ultimately stands on its evidence lives or dies right here, at the research stage. Pick a subject too big and you drown. Trust the wrong source and every later claim inherits its errors. Lose track of where a fact came from and you may have to cut your best evidence or, worse, pass off someone else’s words as your own without ever meaning to.

In this lesson you will take a sprawling subject and scope it down to a case you can actually research — a bounded time, a definite place, a handful of guiding questions. You will learn where to look for sources and how to judge whether any one of them can bear weight. And you will take structured notes that carry their own source with them, so that when you sit down to write, the attribution is already done. By the end you will hold the first real pieces of a technology case study of your own.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to:

  1. Scope a single technology into a researchable case study with a defined time frame, geography, and set of guiding questions.
  2. Distinguish primary from secondary sources and evaluate any source for relevance, reliability, bias, and provenance.
  3. Apply a structured note-taking and source-attribution method that records evidence accurately and avoids plagiarism.

Topic 1 — Scoping a Researchable Technology

Two researchers sit down on the same Monday, and both announce they are “studying the internet.” Three weeks later, one has a folder of scattered articles and a paragraph that says, in effect, the internet is big and important. The other has traced the ARPANET — the U.S. Defense Department’s packet-switching network, from its 1969 debut to the 1983 switch to TCP/IP that seeded the modern internet — and can tell you exactly how four linked computers grew into the spine of a global network, with the documents to prove every step. Same curiosity, opposite results. What separated them was one decision made on that first Monday: scope.

Scope is the deliberate narrowing of a broad subject into a case small enough to research thoroughly and defend with evidence. It trades breadth for depth — instead of saying a little about everything, you say something well-supported about one thing. You set it by fixing three boundaries.

  • Time frame. Choose a defined span rather than “the whole history.” For a printing-press case you might pick 1440–1500, from Gutenberg’s movable type to the spread of print across Europe — not “printing through the ages.”
  • Geography. Pin the case to a place or region. “The bicycle in 1890s Britain” is researchable; “the bicycle, everywhere, always” is not.
  • The specific technology versus its whole family. Decide whether you are studying one device or an entire category. “The safety bicycle” is a case; “wheeled transport” is a library.

With boundaries set, draft guiding questions — the specific questions your research must answer. For a technology case study, build them around the three-part spine of origins, adoption, and impact: Where, when, and why did this technology originate? Who adopted it, how fast, and what stood in the way? What changed because of it, and for whom? These three questions are the backbone the whole capstone will hang on.

Watch a scope tighten and feel the difference. Start with “robots.” That is a subject, not a case — you could chase it for a lifetime and never reach the end. Now squeeze: “the industrial welding robot on U.S. automobile assembly lines, 1961–1985.” All at once the fog lifts. There are patents you can pull, factory installations you can date, companies you can name, and effects on autoworkers you can actually measure. A tight scope does not shrink your ambition; it gives your ambition somewhere to land.

Topic 2 — Finding and Evaluating Sources

Two students stand to defend the same sentence: the telegraph “transformed business.” The first gestures at a blog post found by a quick search. The second reads aloud from an 1866 merchant’s letter, the writer grumbling that he can no longer take a leisurely week to answer an offer — a wire now demands a reply within the hour. Both made the same claim. Only the second can make the room believe it, because only the second went and found a source worth standing on. This topic is about learning to do that on purpose.

Begin with the most important distinction in historical research. A primary source is material produced at the time of the events you are studying, by people who took part in or witnessed them. A secondary source is a later account that interprets, analyzes, or synthesizes those events. The distinction tells you what each can prove: primary sources are your raw evidence; secondary sources are expert interpretations you weigh, lean on, and cite.

  • Primary sources for a technology case include patents, engineering drawings, contemporary newspaper reports, diaries and letters, advertisements, photographs, factory records, sales figures, and government statistics from the period.
  • Secondary sources include scholarly books and journal articles, academic encyclopedias, and well-documented histories written after the fact.

Where do you look? Start with your library’s catalog and its scholarly databases — searchable collections of peer-reviewed articles, such as JSTOR or your institution’s index. Use archives (physical or digitized collections of original documents) for primary material, and reputable reference works (an academic encyclopedia, a museum’s curatorial page) to orient yourself before going deep. Treat an open web search as a starting point that leads you to better sources, not as a destination.

Now the core skill, the one that separates research from collecting: source evaluation, the deliberate judging of whether a source is good enough to build on. Run every candidate through five criteria — every time, no exceptions:

  • Relevance — does it actually bear on your scoped question, or just your broad topic?
  • Reliability — is it accurate and well-supported, or unsourced and error-prone?
  • Author authority — does the author have the expertise or firsthand position to know?
  • Bias and perspective — what viewpoint or interest does the author bring, and how does it shape what they emphasize or omit?
  • Provenance — where did this source come from, who has handled it, and can its origin be traced? A document of unknown origin is a weak foundation.

Put two sources side by side, both making the same claim — “the cotton gin increased cotton production” — and run the five criteria over each. The first is an anonymous, undated web page that states a figure and cites nothing: it fails authority, reliability, and provenance in a single breath. The second is a peer-reviewed economic history that names its author and publisher and footnotes the period export records behind its numbers: it passes all five. The claim may well be true in both. But you can only build on the second one — the first would collapse under the first hard question.

Finally, never rest a major claim on a single source. Triangulation is the practice of confirming a claim across two or more independent sources. If a patent, a period newspaper, and a modern scholarly history all agree the safety bicycle reached mass adoption in the 1890s, your claim is solid; if only one source says so, you flag it as uncertain. Independent agreement is what turns a fact you found into a fact you can defend.

Topic 3 — Structured Notes and Honest Attribution

It is two weeks from now. You are deep in a draft, and there it is in your notes — the perfect fact, the one that nails your point. You go to cite it and stop cold: you have no idea where it came from. Now you face an ugly little choice. Cut the best evidence you found, or use it and quietly risk passing off someone else’s work as your own. An undocumented note is not a half-victory; at this moment it is worse than no note at all. The cure is to make every note carry its own source from the instant you write it.

Use a structured note — a record that pairs every captured fact with the full source and the exact location it came from. Because the citation travels with the fact, attribution later becomes automatic; there is nothing to reconstruct. Capture, for every note:

  1. The fact, quotation, or idea itself.
  2. The full source: author, title, publisher or publication, and date.
  3. The exact location: page number, archive box, URL, or timestamp.
  4. The note type (see below): quotation, paraphrase, or summary.

That last field matters because there are three ways to record someone else’s material, and you must know which you used:

  • A quotation reproduces the source’s exact words, inside quotation marks.
  • A paraphrase restates one passage in your own words and sentence structure.
  • A summary compresses a longer passage into a brief account in your own words.

All three must be attributed. The difference is mechanical: a quotation needs quotation marks and a citation; a paraphrase and a summary need a citation but no quotation marks.

This is the crack through which plagiarism — presenting someone else’s words, ideas, or structure as your own without attribution — slips in, and it almost always slips in by accident rather than design. Two failure modes do most of the damage. Patchwriting swaps a few words of a source while keeping its sentence skeleton intact, then calls the result a paraphrase; it is still plagiarism, because the structure and the ideas were borrowed whole. The “forgot the citation” failure mode is gentler and just as ruinous: you take a clean, accurate note, neglect to record where it came from, and weeks later present it as your own — never knowing you did.

Here is a structured note done right, for a paraphrase:

Fact: The safety bicycle’s chain drive and equal-sized wheels made cycling accessible to ordinary riders, unlike the dangerous high-wheel design. Source: Smith, Jane. The Bicycle in Victorian Britain. Oxford University Press, 2015. Location: p. 84. Type: Paraphrase.

With that note in hand, the citation writes itself when you compose, and there is no path by which the idea silently becomes “yours.”

Activity — “Scope and Source Your Technology”

  • Type: Individual exercise on the learner’s own capstone project (application).
  • Time: 25 minutes.
  • Description: You will take the first real step on your own capstone. You will name the technology you intend to research, scope it into a researchable case, find one genuine source for it, evaluate that source against the five criteria, and record one attribution-ready structured note — leaving this lesson with a working foundation rather than just an idea.

Instructions:

  1. (3 min) Name your technology and write it at the top of your page.
  2. (7 min) Draft a scoped case statement: a time frame, a geography, and three guiding questions covering origins, adoption, and impact. Aim for a scope you could research in a few focused sessions.
  3. (8 min) Find one real source for your case using a library catalog, a scholarly database, or a reputable reference work. Label it primary or secondary.
  4. (5 min) Evaluate your source against the five criteria — relevance, reliability, author authority, bias/perspective, provenance — writing one short line for each.
  5. (2 min) Record one structured note from the source with all four fields: fact, full source, exact location, and note type (quotation, paraphrase, or summary).

Wrap-Up and Reflection

You arrived with a topic you were curious about; you leave with the bones of a real research project — a scoped case, an evaluated source, and an attribution-ready note. The core lesson is that good historical research is front-loaded: the discipline you spend now on narrowing, judging, and documenting is what makes a defensible argument possible at all. Scope tight, source critically, note honestly.

Those structured notes are not busywork. They are the evidence base you will draw on directly: each note you take with its source attached is one less claim you will have to scramble to support later, and one less chance of accidentally borrowing someone else’s words. A thesis-driven written case study is only as strong as the notes beneath it.

Key Takeaways

  • A researchable case is a broad subject narrowed by time frame, geography, and guiding questions built around origins, adoption, and impact.
  • Primary sources are period evidence; secondary sources are later interpretation — and both must be judged, not trusted on sight.
  • Evaluate every source for relevance, reliability, author authority, bias, and provenance, and triangulate important claims across independent sources.
  • A structured note pairs each fact with its full source, exact location, and note type, making honest attribution automatic.
  • Plagiarism is usually accidental — patchwriting and the forgotten citation are the main culprits, and disciplined notes prevent both.

Reflection Questions

  1. Think of a time you looked something up and accepted it without checking the source. Running the five criteria over it now, would it have survived — and what would you do differently?
  2. Take a technology you use every day and draft a scope for it in one sentence: a time frame, a place, and one guiding question that you could actually research.
  3. Suppose two of your sources flatly contradict each other on a key fact about your technology. Using the evaluation criteria and triangulation, how would you decide which to believe, or whether to trust either?
