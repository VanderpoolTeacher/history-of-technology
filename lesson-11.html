Lesson 11: Networks and the Internet

Module: 6 — The Internet Age · Hours: 3 (180 min)

Introduction

Picture a room full of powerful computers, each one humming away on its own desk, and none of them able to say a single word to any of the others. Each machine is an island. To send a report from one to the next, you would copy it onto a disk and physically walk it across the room.

That isolation was once the normal state of computing, and it set a hard ceiling on what machines could do together. Without a way to interconnect independently built and independently run networks, knowledge stayed trapped wherever it was created, and collaboration stopped at the edge of the building. The problem was not raw computing power — it was connection.

In this lesson you will trace the engineering ideas that turned isolated machines into a single, planet-spanning network of networks: packet switching, the early research network that proved it, and the shared protocols that let any network join in. You will also see the first social effects of being able to reach anyone, anywhere, who was also connected.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to:

  1. Define packet switching and explain how it differs from circuit switching as a way to move data through a shared network.
  2. Describe the development of ARPANET and the TCP/IP protocols, and explain how they turned separate networks into an internetwork — a “network of networks.”
  3. Explain how open, layered protocols and a decentralized design enabled the internet to grow rapidly without central control.
  4. Analyze the early social effects of internet access — email, newsgroups, and online community — on communication, collaboration, and the spread of information.

Topic 1 — Packets, Not Circuits

Start with a question that has a surprising answer: when you send a message across a shared network, how should that network move it? The obvious approach — open a private line from sender to receiver — turns out to be exactly the wrong one, and seeing why is the foundation of everything that follows.

The older approach is circuit switching — a method of communication in which the network reserves a dedicated, end-to-end path for the entire duration of a conversation, exactly the way a traditional telephone call works. What it does is guarantee a private channel: once the circuit is set up, the two ends have it all to themselves. The trouble is what that costs. The reserved line sits idle whenever no one is talking, wasting capacity, and if any single link along that fixed path is cut, the whole conversation drops instantly — there is no path left.

The alternative is packet switching — a method that breaks a message into small, individually addressed pieces called packets, each carrying the destination address and its position in the sequence, routes each packet independently across whatever links are free, and reassembles them in order at the destination. What packet switching does is make the network robust and efficient at the same time. It is robust because packets route around damage or congestion: if one link goes down, later packets simply take another path. It is efficient because many separate conversations share the same links instead of each demanding a reserved line of its own.

Here is the idea made concrete. Imagine you need to send a long letter and, instead of mailing it as one bundle, you write it across fifty numbered postcards and drop them in the mail. Each postcard finds its own route through the postal system; some go one way, some another; and the recipient sorts them back into order by their numbers. If one road is closed, the postcards on later trips simply go around it — nothing is lost, because no single road had to stay open the whole time. Packet switching does this for data.

Topic 2 — ARPANET and TCP/IP

Packet switching is a good idea on paper. The next question is whether it works at scale, and then a harder one: once several separate packet networks exist, how do you get them to talk to each other? This topic follows that arc from one network to a network of networks.

The first large-scale proof was ARPANET — the first major packet-switched network, built to link research laboratories and university sites so they could share computing resources and results. What ARPANET did was demonstrate, in the real world, that packet switching could carry genuine traffic reliably between distant sites. But its success created a new problem. Soon other packet-switched networks existed too, each built by a different organization with its own rules — and they could not understand one another. You had many islands again, just larger ones.

The solution was a shared language every network could agree to speak: TCP/IP, short for Transmission Control Protocol / Internet Protocol — a pair of cooperating protocols that together let any network join a larger whole. The Internet Protocol (IP) gives every machine an address and handles routing each packet toward that address, hop by hop. The Transmission Control Protocol (TCP) sits on top of IP and handles reliability: it numbers the packets, checks that they all arrive, asks for any that go missing, and reassembles them in the correct order. Because TCP/IP did not care what kind of network sat underneath it, any network that adopted it could plug in. That is what creates an internetwork — a network of networks, in which many independently run networks interconnect into one larger system.

A useful marker for when the modern internet’s architecture was set is the 1983 cutover, when ARPANET formally switched over to TCP/IP as its required protocol. After that point, the design principle was fixed: speak TCP/IP, and you can join. As a concrete illustration, picture two separate campus networks, each working fine internally but mutually deaf. Place a single device — a router, a machine that forwards packets between networks using their IP addresses — between them, have both networks speak TCP/IP, and the two instantly become one reachable system. Repeat that move thousands of times and you have the internet.

Topic 3 — Why It Could Grow Without a Boss

Here is something genuinely strange about the internet: it grew from a handful of sites to a global system, yet no company, government, or committee ever ran it the way an airline runs its schedule. How can a system that huge have no one in charge of it? The answer is in how it was designed, and it explains why the growth was explosive rather than gradual.

The first design choice is layered design — an architecture in which functions are stacked in layers, so that applications sit on top of TCP/IP rather than being built into it. What layering does is decouple uses from plumbing. The core network only has to move packets; anything you want to do with those packets — send mail, transfer a file, browse pages — is a separate layer built on top. This means a new use can appear without anyone touching or asking permission to change the underlying network. The plumbing never has to know what flows through it.

The second choice is open standards — publicly available specifications that anyone is free to implement, distributed as documents called RFCs (Requests for Comments), the open technical notes in which internet protocols are proposed and defined. What open standards do is prevent ownership: because the rules are public and free to implement, no single vendor or government controls the network or can lock others out. The third choice is decentralization — the absence of any central switchboard or authority that must grant permission to connect. Because there is no central control point, a new network can join simply by speaking the agreed protocols and plugging in; it does not file an application with anyone.

Put those three together and the result is exponential, bottom-up growth. Each new network adds value for everyone already connected, and joining requires no permission — so growth feeds itself. As a concrete illustration, contrast this with a system that does have a boss: to add a new branch to a private corporate network, you must request access, get provisioned, and be approved. On the internet, by design, there was no one to ask. A university could connect its network on a Tuesday and reach the rest of the world by Wednesday.

Topic 4 — Early Life Online

Infrastructure only matters because of what people do with it. Before the network reached the general public, the first communities of connected users were already discovering that reaching anyone, anywhere, instantly, changes how people work and relate. This topic looks at that first wave of human experience.

The first killer application — a use so compelling that it alone justifies adopting the technology — was email, short for electronic mail: asynchronous, cheap, many-to-many text messages sent between connected users. What email did was collapse the cost and delay of distance. You could write to a colleague across the country, they could read and reply whenever they chose, and the message cost effectively nothing to send. Communication no longer required both people to be present at the same moment, as a phone call did, nor to wait days, as a letter did.

Around email grew the first online communities. Newsgroups and bulletin boards were shared, topic-organized message spaces where anyone connected could post a question or answer and everyone could read the thread, and mailing lists delivered group discussions to every subscriber’s inbox. What these did was create shared knowledge spaces that were not tied to any one place: a question posted by someone in one city could be answered by a stranger in another, and the answer stayed visible for everyone who came later. The early social effects were immediate. Distance collapsed; research collaboration sped up because findings and questions circulated in hours rather than weeks; and brand-new norms — and frictions — appeared around text-only communication, where tone is easy to misread and a careless message can spark a public argument.

As a concrete illustration, imagine a researcher stuck on a technical problem posts it to a newsgroup at midnight. By morning, three people in three different countries — none of whom she has ever met — have replied with partial answers, and a fourth has linked the whole thread to a related discussion. That kind of fast, distributed, stranger-to-stranger help simply had no equivalent before the network existed.

Activity — “Route the Packets”

  • Type: Group exercise (hands-on application and analysis, not recall).
  • Time: 40 minutes.
  • Description: You and your group will act as the network itself. Given a small map of nodes connected by links, with one link marked “down,” you will split a message into packets, route each packet to the destination around the failure, reassemble them in order, and then explain why a circuit-switched call on the same map would have failed. A second round adds congestion on a link to force you to re-route, showing how the same design copes with overload as well as failure.

Instructions:

  1. (5 min) In groups of three or four, take the supplied node map. It shows six labeled nodes — A, B, C, D, E, and F — connected by links: A–B, A–C, B–D, C–D, C–E, D–F, and E–F. The link B–D is marked “down.” Your message must travel from node A to node F.
  2. (10 min) Write a short message (one sentence) and break it into exactly four numbered packets, one or two words each, labeling every packet with its destination (F) and its sequence number (1 of 4, 2 of 4, and so on). For each packet, write the full path of nodes it will travel from A to F. You may send different packets by different routes; you may not use the downed B–D link.
  3. (10 min) Hand your packets, in scrambled order, to a neighboring group acting as node F. They must reassemble your message using only the sequence numbers, then report it back to you. Confirm it reassembled correctly.
  4. (10 min) Round two: the instructor now marks the link C–D “congested” (still usable but to be avoided). Re-route any packets that used C–D onto a different valid path and record the new routes.
  5. (5 min) As a group, write one or two sentences answering: why would a single circuit-switched call from A to F have failed on this map, and how did packet switching survive both the failure and the congestion?

Wrap-Up and Reflection

You arrived able to picture computers as isolated islands; you leave able to explain exactly how they were joined into one global system. The core message is that the internet was made possible not by faster machines but by a set of design choices — break messages into independently routed packets, give every machine an address, agree on a shared and open protocol, and let any network join without permission. Each choice traded away central control and guaranteed private lines in exchange for robustness, sharing, and the freedom to grow.

That trade is the most valuable idea to carry forward. Once you see the internet as a network of networks held together by open, layered, decentralized protocols rather than by any owner or switchboard, its explosive growth and its first social effects — instant, distance-free, stranger- to-stranger communication — stop being mysterious and start looking inevitable.

Key Takeaways

  • Packet switching breaks messages into independently routed, addressed packets, making the network robust to failure and efficient through sharing — unlike a fragile, reserved circuit.
  • ARPANET proved packet switching at scale; TCP/IP gave separate networks a shared language, turning them into an internetwork — a network of networks.
  • The Internet Protocol addresses and routes packets; the Transmission Control Protocol makes delivery reliable and reassembles packets in order.
  • Layered design, open standards (RFCs), and decentralization let the internet grow bottom-up and exponentially, with no owner and no central permission point.
  • The first applications — email, newsgroups, mailing lists — collapsed distance and created the first online communities, with both new benefits and new frictions.

Reflection Questions

  1. Think of a message you sent today across a network. Trace what it would have taken to send the same information in a world of isolated, unconnected machines — and estimate how much slower your day would have been.
  2. The lesson argues that the internet grew because no one owned it. Where else, inside or outside technology, do you see open and decentralized systems growing faster than tightly controlled ones — and where does the lack of a central authority cause problems instead?
  3. Apply the packet-switching idea to a non-digital system not discussed here — for example, routing emergency supplies across a road network after a disaster. How would breaking the shipment into independently routed pieces make delivery more robust than reserving one fixed route?
