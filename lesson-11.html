Lesson 11: Networks and the Internet

Module: 6 — The Internet Age · Hours: 3 (180 min)

Introduction

Picture a room full of powerful computers in 1965, each humming away on its own desk, and not one of them able to say a single word to any of the others. Each machine is an island. To carry a report from one to the next, you copy it onto a reel of tape and walk it across the room by hand.

That isolation was once the ordinary state of computing, and it set a hard ceiling on what machines could do together. With no way to interconnect independently built and independently run networks, knowledge stayed trapped wherever it was created, and collaboration stopped at the edge of the building. The bottleneck was never raw computing power — it was connection.

In this lesson you will trace the engineering ideas that turned isolated machines into a single, planet-spanning network of networks: packet switching, the early research network that proved it, and the shared protocols that let any network join in. You will also see the first social effects of being able to reach anyone, anywhere, who was also connected.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to:

  1. Define packet switching and explain how it differs from circuit switching as a way to move data through a shared network.
  2. Describe the development of ARPANET and the TCP/IP protocols, and explain how they turned separate networks into an internetwork — a “network of networks.”
  3. Explain how open, layered protocols and a decentralized design enabled the internet to grow rapidly without central control.
  4. Analyze the early social effects of internet access — email, newsgroups, and online community — on communication, collaboration, and the spread of information.

Topic 1 — Packets, Not Circuits

In the early 1960s, an engineer named Paul Baran sat at the RAND Corporation working a grim problem: how do you build a communications network that keeps working even after pieces of it have been destroyed? The telephone system of his day could not. And in answering that question, Baran — and, independently, a British physicist named Donald Davies, who coined the word that stuck — landed on the idea underneath everything you will study in this lesson. Seeing why the obvious approach fails is the foundation of everything that follows.

The obvious approach is circuit switching — a method in which the network reserves a dedicated, end-to-end path for the entire duration of a conversation, exactly the way a traditional telephone call works. Pick up the receiver, dial, and the system stitches together a private channel that is yours alone until you hang up. That privacy is precisely the weakness. The reserved line sits idle every moment no one is speaking, squandering capacity; worse, snip any single link along that fixed path and the whole conversation drops on the spot, because no other path was ever held in reserve. Baran’s wartime worry was exactly this fragility.

His alternative was packet switching — break a message into small, individually addressed pieces called packets, stamp each one with the destination address and its place in the sequence, fling each across whatever links happen to be free, and reassemble them in order at the far end. Two virtues fall out at once. The network becomes robust, because packets route around damage or congestion: knock out a link, and later packets simply choose another. And it becomes efficient, because many separate conversations share the same wires instead of each hoarding a private line.

Picture it as mail. You need to send a long letter, but instead of one fat envelope you copy it across fifty numbered postcards and drop them in a box. Each postcard finds its own way through the postal system — some north, some south — and the recipient sorts them back into order by their numbers. Close one road and the later postcards detour around it; nothing is lost, because no single road ever had to stay open the whole time. Packet switching does exactly this for data.

Topic 2 — ARPANET and TCP/IP

Late in 1969, in a lab at the University of California, Los Angeles, a graduate student tried to log in to a computer four hundred miles away at the Stanford Research Institute. He began typing the word LOGIN. The system crashed after two letters. The first message ever sent over the new research network was, accidentally, “lo” — and yet that stuttering link was the moment packet switching left the page and started moving real traffic between distant machines. The harder question came next: once several separate packet networks existed, how could you get them to talk to each other?

That first network was ARPANET — the first major packet-switched network, funded by the U.S. Defense Department’s Advanced Research Projects Agency to link laboratories and university sites so they could share scarce computing resources and results. It proved, in the field rather than on a whiteboard, that packet switching could carry genuine traffic reliably across the country. But success bred a fresh problem. Soon other packet networks existed too, each built by a different organization to its own rules — and they could not understand one another. The islands were back, just larger.

The fix was a shared language every network could agree to speak: TCP/IP, short for Transmission Control Protocol / Internet Protocol, the pair of cooperating protocols designed chiefly by Vint Cerf and Robert Kahn. The Internet Protocol (IP) gives every machine an address and shoves each packet toward that address, one hop at a time. The Transmission Control Protocol (TCP) rides on top of IP and minds reliability: it numbers the packets, checks that they all arrive, demands re-sends of any that vanish, and reassembles the rest in order. Crucially, TCP/IP does not care what kind of network sits beneath it — so any network that adopted it could simply plug in. That is what creates an internetwork: a network of networks, in which many independently run networks interconnect into one larger system.

The architecture locked into place on a single date. On January 1, 1983, ARPANET formally cut over to TCP/IP as its required protocol, and from that day the design principle was fixed — speak TCP/IP, and you can join. Picture two campus networks, each humming along internally yet mutually deaf. Drop a single device between them — a router, a machine that forwards packets between networks by reading their IP addresses — have both speak TCP/IP, and the two snap into one reachable system. Repeat that move a few thousand times and you have the internet.

Topic 3 — Why It Could Grow Without a Boss

There is no head office of the internet. No chief executive signs off on new connections, no central switchboard routes the world’s traffic, no committee approves your right to plug in. A system spanning the planet and carrying the affairs of billions has, in the ordinary sense, nobody in charge — and that is not an oversight. It is a design, and the design is exactly why the growth was explosive rather than gradual.

The first choice is layered design — an architecture in which functions are stacked in layers, so that applications sit on top of TCP/IP rather than being baked into it. Layering pries apart uses from plumbing. The core network has one humble job, moving packets; everything you might actually do with those packets — send mail, transfer a file, browse pages — lives in a separate layer above. A wholly new use can therefore appear without anyone touching the network beneath it or asking leave to do so. The plumbing never needs to know what flows through it.

The second choice is open standards — publicly available specifications anyone is free to implement, circulated as documents called RFCs (Requests for Comments). The very name, chosen by Steve Crocker for the first such note in 1969, signals the spirit: these are open invitations to comment, not commandments from above. Because the rules are public and free to build on, no single vendor or government owns the network or can bar anyone from it. The third choice is decentralization — the plain absence of any central authority whose permission you must seek. A new network joins by speaking the agreed protocols and plugging in; it files an application with no one.

Stack those three together and growth feeds itself. Every new network makes the whole more valuable to everyone already on it, and the price of joining is merely agreement to the protocols — so the thing snowballs. Set that beside a system that does have a boss: to add a branch to a private corporate network, you request access, wait to be provisioned, and hope to be approved. On the internet, by design, there was no one to ask. A university could wire up its network on a Tuesday and reach the far side of the world by Wednesday.

Topic 4 — Early Life Online

The engineers who built ARPANET expected its great use to be sharing expensive computers across distance. What they had not quite expected was that the people using it would mostly want to talk to one another. By the early 1970s, a programmer named Ray Tomlinson had wired up a way to send messages between machines and reached for a character to separate the user from the host — the @ sign, until then nearly useless on a keyboard. Within a couple of years, this side project was generating more traffic than anything the network was officially built to do. Infrastructure, it turns out, only matters because of what people insist on doing with it.

That runaway side project was the first killer application — a use so compelling it alone justifies adopting the whole technology — and it was email, short for electronic mail: asynchronous, cheap, many-to-many text messages between connected users. Email crushed the cost and the delay of distance at one stroke. Write to a colleague across the country, and they read and answer whenever they please, for effectively nothing. Communication no longer demanded that both people be present in the same instant, as a phone call did, nor that one wait days, as a letter did.

Around email clustered the first online communities. Newsgroups and bulletin boards were shared, topic-organized message spaces where anyone connected could post a question or an answer and everyone could read the thread; mailing lists pushed group discussions straight into every subscriber’s inbox. These built shared knowledge that belonged to no single place. A question posted by someone in one city might be answered by a stranger in another, and the answer lingered, visible to everyone who arrived later. The social effects landed fast. Distance collapsed. Research sped up as findings circulated in hours rather than weeks. And entirely new manners — and frictions — sprang up around text-only talk, where tone is easy to misread and one careless line can ignite a public quarrel; the early users even had a word for those eruptions, flame wars.

Make it concrete. A researcher stuck on a stubborn problem posts it to a newsgroup at midnight. By morning three people in three countries — none of whom she has ever met — have answered in part, and a fourth has linked her thread to a related discussion across the ocean. That kind of fast, distributed, stranger-to-stranger help had no equivalent before the network existed.

Activity — “Route the Packets”

  • Type: Group exercise (hands-on application and analysis, not recall).
  • Time: 40 minutes.
  • Description: You and your group will act as the network itself. Given a small map of nodes connected by links, with one link marked “down,” you will split a message into packets, route each packet to the destination around the failure, reassemble them in order, and then explain why a circuit-switched call on the same map would have failed. A second round adds congestion on a link to force you to re-route, showing how the same design copes with overload as well as failure.

Instructions:

  1. (5 min) In groups of three or four, take the supplied node map. It shows six labeled nodes — A, B, C, D, E, and F — connected by links: A–B, A–C, B–D, C–D, C–E, D–F, and E–F. The link B–D is marked “down.” Your message must travel from node A to node F.
  2. (10 min) Write a short message (one sentence) and break it into exactly four numbered packets, one or two words each, labeling every packet with its destination (F) and its sequence number (1 of 4, 2 of 4, and so on). For each packet, write the full path of nodes it will travel from A to F. You may send different packets by different routes; you may not use the downed B–D link.
  3. (10 min) Hand your packets, in scrambled order, to a neighboring group acting as node F. They must reassemble your message using only the sequence numbers, then report it back to you. Confirm it reassembled correctly.
  4. (10 min) Round two: the instructor now marks the link C–D “congested” (still usable but to be avoided). Re-route any packets that used C–D onto a different valid path and record the new routes.
  5. (5 min) As a group, write one or two sentences answering: why would a single circuit-switched call from A to F have failed on this map, and how did packet switching survive both the failure and the congestion?

Wrap-Up and Reflection

You arrived able to picture computers as isolated islands; you leave able to explain exactly how they were joined into one global system. The core message is that the internet was made possible not by faster machines but by a set of design choices — break messages into independently routed packets, give every machine an address, agree on a shared and open protocol, and let any network join without permission. Each choice traded away central control and guaranteed private lines in exchange for robustness, sharing, and the freedom to grow.

That trade is the most valuable idea to carry forward. Once you see the internet as a network of networks held together by open, layered, decentralized protocols rather than by any owner or switchboard, its explosive growth and its first social effects — instant, distance-free, stranger- to-stranger communication — stop being mysterious and start looking inevitable.

Key Takeaways

  • Packet switching breaks messages into independently routed, addressed packets, making the network robust to failure and efficient through sharing — unlike a fragile, reserved circuit.
  • ARPANET proved packet switching at scale; TCP/IP gave separate networks a shared language, turning them into an internetwork — a network of networks.
  • The Internet Protocol addresses and routes packets; the Transmission Control Protocol makes delivery reliable and reassembles packets in order.
  • Layered design, open standards (RFCs), and decentralization let the internet grow bottom-up and exponentially, with no owner and no central permission point.
  • The first applications — email, newsgroups, mailing lists — collapsed distance and created the first online communities, with both new benefits and new frictions.

Reflection Questions

  1. Think of a message you sent today across a network. Trace what it would have taken to send the same information in a world of isolated, unconnected machines — and estimate how much slower your day would have been.
  2. The lesson argues that the internet grew because no one owned it. Where else, inside or outside technology, do you see open and decentralized systems growing faster than tightly controlled ones — and where does the lack of a central authority cause problems instead?
  3. Apply the packet-switching idea to a non-digital system not discussed here — for example, routing emergency supplies across a road network after a disaster. How would breaking the shipment into independently routed pieces make delivery more robust than reserving one fixed route?
