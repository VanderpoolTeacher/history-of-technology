Lesson 12: The Web, Mobile, and Ubiquitous Computing

Module: 6 — The Internet Age · Hours: 3 (180 min)

Introduction

Reach into your pocket. The device resting there can summon almost any fact ever written down, place a stranger’s face in front of you in real time from the far side of an ocean, and pay for your coffee — all while quietly noting where you are standing and what you last tapped. For most of the world, life now unfolds inside that always-on connection; being offline has become the unusual state, the thing you have to arrange on purpose.

Yet step back only a single lifetime and almost no one was connected to a global network at all. The distance between that world and this one is enormous, and the surprising thing is how quickly it was crossed — and on how few inventions the crossing turned. Two of them, arriving barely fifteen years apart, did most of the work.

The first, in the early 1990s, was a way to browse the network rather than merely send data across it — a system of linked pages, laid on top of the decades-old internet, that ordinary people could navigate without being specialists. The second, in 2007, was a computer small enough to live in a pocket, which turned the connection from something you sat down at a desk to use into something you carried everywhere, every waking hour. Mistake how recent and how deliberately engineered this shift was, and the always-on world looks like natural progress — when it was in fact a transformation with clear winners and clear losers.

In this lesson you will first pin down a distinction almost everyone blurs: the difference between the World Wide Web and the internet it rides upon. Then you will trace the rise of the smartphone and ubiquitous, always-on computing, along with the platforms and social media that grew on top of it. Finally, you will weigh the whole transition on a ledger — who benefited, who bore the costs, and across which human, ethical, and environmental dimensions.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to:

  1. Distinguish the World Wide Web from the internet, defining the Web as a hypertext information system (browsers, hyperlinks, URLs) running on top of the internet.
  2. Describe the rise of the smartphone and mobile, ubiquitous, “always-on” computing, and explain how it embedded networked devices into everyday life.
  3. Explain how social media and platform services reshaped how people communicate, form communities, and access information.
  4. Evaluate who benefited and who bore the costs of the networked, always-on transition — across human, ethical, and environmental dimensions. (C10)

Topic 1 — The Web Is Not the Internet

In March 1989, a software engineer at CERN, the European particle-physics laboratory near Geneva, handed his boss a modest proposal for managing the lab’s tangle of documents. The boss scribbled “vague, but exciting” across the top and let him pursue it. The engineer was Tim Berners-Lee, and the thing he went on to build — releasing it to the public in 1991 — was the World Wide Web. Notice what already existed by then: the internet, twenty years old and humming along. Berners-Lee did not invent the internet. He built something new on top of it, and confusing the two is the single most important error to avoid in this lesson, because nearly every later idea depends on keeping them apart.

So separate them cleanly. The internet is the underlying global network — the plumbing of cables, routers, and addresses that shuttles data between machines, the lower layer that simply delivers packets anywhere they are addressed. The World Wide Web (WWW) is a different beast entirely: one application that runs on top of the internet, a hypertext information system for publishing and browsing linked documents. It sits alongside other applications, such as email, that also ride the same internet yet are emphatically not the Web. The Web leans on the internet to carry its data, but the internet was moving other traffic for two decades before the Web existed and would keep right on working if the Web vanished tomorrow. The Web rides on the internet; the two are not the same thing.

Berners-Lee made information browsable with a small kit of parts, most of which he wrote himself in 1990. Hypertext is text studded with hyperlinks — clickable connections that leap you from one document to another. Pages are written in HTML (HyperText Markup Language), which structures their text and links. A browser and a server trade those pages using HTTP (HyperText Transfer Protocol), the agreed rules of the exchange. Each page is found by its URL (Uniform Resource Locator), a unique address on the Web. A browser is the program that fetches a page by its URL, renders the HTML, and follows the links you click; a server is the machine that stores pages and ships them on request. Together these parts turned a network only specialists could navigate into something anyone could click through.

The Web did not freeze in 1991. The earliest version was largely read-only — a handful of authors published pages and nearly everyone else merely read them. The breakthrough into the mainstream came in 1993 with Mosaic, the first browser to show images inline alongside text and run easily on ordinary personal computers; suddenly the Web looked like something, not just read like something. Later the medium shifted again, to what is often called Web 2.0, an interactive Web defined by user-generated content, where ordinary people — not just publishers — create the material: posts, reviews, videos, profiles. Trace why this drove mass adoption. Before browsers, reaching information on the network meant typing arcane commands and knowing precisely where things lived; after Mosaic, a person who had never programmed anything could open a page, click a blue underlined word, and land somewhere new. That one act — click a link, arrive somewhere — is what finally pulled the general public onto a network that had sat there, unbrowsable, for years.

Topic 2 — Computing in Your Pocket

On a San Francisco stage in January 2007, Steve Jobs told the audience Apple would unveil three new products: a widescreen iPod, a phone, and a breakthrough internet device. He paused, repeated the list, and then delivered the punchline — these were not three devices but one, the iPhone. The joke carried the whole argument of this topic. For decades you went somewhere to use a computer; the device Jobs held up that day was about to make the network something you simply carried, everywhere, all the time. That structural change, not the convenience, is the point.

The engine of the shift was the smartphone — a convergence device that folds what used to be a drawer full of separate machines into one always-connected object: a phone, a camera, a GPS (Global Positioning System) receiver for location, a music player, and a full internet client, all in a pocket. Convergence did two things at once: it collapsed many gadgets into a single one and kept that one continuously online. You stopped choosing a device for each task and started carrying one device that did nearly everything and was never offline.

Two further developments turned the connection from occasional to constant. Mobile networks — wireless data networks that follow you as you move — meant getting online no longer required plugging in at a fixed spot. App ecosystems — curated marketplaces of small, single-purpose programs called apps (applications), opened to outside developers in 2008 — meant fresh capabilities could land on the device any time, with a tap. Between them, going online stopped being an event you “sat down” to do and became a background condition of waking life. This is ubiquitous computing, also called “always-on” computing — a state in which networked sensors and devices are woven so thoroughly into everyday environments that you stop noticing them: the watch counting your steps, the doorbell streaming video, the speaker waiting for its name.

Set two mornings side by side. In the desk era, to read a message or look something up you walked to a machine, sat down, connected, did the task, and got up — computing was a place you went. In the ubiquitous era you glance at a wrist between strides, a sensor logs your run though no one asked it to, and a notification finds you while you wait in a checkout line — computing is an ambient condition you are never quite outside of. Nothing about that second morning required you to “use a computer,” and yet you spent all of it inside a networked system.

Topic 3 — Platforms, Social Media, and Information

A single phone in a single pocket is a tool. A billion of them, all carried by people who can now publish to one another instantly, is a society waiting to be rearranged — and in the years after 2007, a small number of services rushed in to do the rearranging. Before long, a startling share of the world’s communication, community, and news ran through a handful of them. This topic describes that shift — what changed about how people connect and learn — and deliberately holds its judgment for the ledger to come.

The dominant form it took is social media — online platforms where users create and share content and connect with one another, now a primary way people communicate and form community. The defining move of a platform is to intermediate. Instead of reaching each other directly, people increasingly pass their online lives through services that sit in the middle, host the content, and quietly decide what each person sees. Why did a mere few of these come to tower over the rest? The network effect — the property by which a service grows more valuable to each user as more people join, because the very people you want to reach are already there. It is a self-feeding loop: the biggest platform is the most useful because it is the biggest, which makes it bigger still. So online life pooled into a few giant platforms rather than spreading evenly across many.

What this did to information cuts both ways, and the honest task is to lay out both edges. On one side, platforms handed ordinary people instant global reach — anyone could publish to a potential audience of millions, for nothing. On the other, the identical machinery bred new troubles: virality, where content spreads explosively whether or not it is true; filter bubbles, where a person mostly meets views that echo their own because the platform feeds them whatever holds their attention; and misinformation, false content that travels as easily as true content — often faster, since it is built to provoke. Hold both halves in one picture. A single post by an unknown person can reach millions overnight, a genuinely new and democratizing power — and that same mechanism can carry a falsehood just as far, just as fast, to just as many people who never see the correction.

Topic 4 — The Ledger: Who Benefited, Who Paid

Two facts about the always-on world sit only inches apart and rarely get held together. The same phone that lets a grandmother in one country see her grandchild’s face in another, for free, is also logging her location every few minutes for a company she will never meet. Both are true at once. The discipline of this topic is to refuse the lazy summary that “technology changed society,” and instead name the specific groups on each side of a ledger: who gained, who paid, and in what currency — human, ethical, or environmental. (C10)

Begin with the benefits, because they are real and large. The networked, always-on transition gave billions of people access to information and services that were once scarce or gated; new livelihoods and markets, as people built businesses, audiences, and incomes that could not have existed before; and connection across distance, letting families, collaborators, and communities stay in contact regardless of geography. These gains are not evenly distributed, but they are genuine, and an honest ledger names them first.

Against those gains stand costs that fall into two groups. The human and ethical costs include surveillance and the erosion of privacy — the always-on, always-sensing device that delivers service also continuously collects data about you; the attention economy — platforms engineered to maximize engagement, capturing and holding your attention because attention is what they sell; the digital divide — the gap between those who have reliable, affordable connectivity and devices and those who do not, which now compounds other inequalities; and gig / platform labor — precarious, often unprotected work mediated by platforms, where the worker bears the risk and the platform takes a cut. (C10) The environmental costs include e-waste — the mountains of discarded, hard-to-recycle devices produced by short replacement cycles — and the energy and water demands of data centers, the large facilities that power the always-on world and that consume substantial electricity and water for cooling. (C10)

The synthesis that ties this together is that every benefit has a distribution — it lands on specific people, and so does every cost. As a concrete, fully worked ledger row to model the method: consider a free messaging app. Benefit: users gain free, instant communication across distance (winners: the billions of people who could not previously afford long-distance contact). Human/ethical cost: the service is free because user data and attention are the product (cost-bearers: users whose private behavior is monetized; the mechanism is data collected from every message and tap). Environmental cost: delivering and storing all those messages runs on power-hungry data centers (cost-bearers: communities near those facilities and the broader climate; the mechanism is the energy and water footprint). Notice that this row names specific groups on each side and a specific mechanism for each entry — never “society” in general.

Activity — “Audit the Always-On World”

  • Type: Case study (group analysis and evaluation, not recall).
  • Time: 40 minutes.
  • Description: Your group will take one everyday networked service and put it on trial as a consequences auditor. You will build a two-column ledger — who benefited and who bore the costs — with at least one human, one ethical, and one environmental entry, each tied to a specific mechanism. Then you will rank which cost you judge most consequential and defend that choice to the room.

Instructions:

  1. (3 min) In groups of three or four, take one assigned service. Options:
    • A free social media app (you pay nothing; the service earns from advertising).
    • A ride-hailing platform (an app connects riders with drivers for paid trips).
    • A smart-home device (a voice speaker or video doorbell connected to a network).
  2. (15 min) Build a two-column ledger headed Who Benefited and Who Bore the Costs. Fill in at least: one benefit (with the specific group that gains), and three costs — one human, one ethical, and one environmental — each naming the specific group that pays and the mechanism (what data is collected, what labor is used, what device or energy footprint is involved). Write the mechanism explicitly for every entry.
  3. (10 min) Rank your three costs from most to least consequential. Write one or two sentences defending your top-ranked cost: why is it the most serious, and for whom?
  4. (12 min) Each group presents its service, its ledger, and its top-ranked cost in 90 seconds. The rest of the room listens for which groups keep appearing as winners and which keep appearing as cost-bearers.

Wrap-Up and Reflection

You arrived treating “the internet” and “being online” as one undifferentiated thing; you leave able to separate the network from the Web that rides on it, to name the shift from computing-as-a- place to computing-as-an-ambient-condition, and — most importantly — to evaluate that whole transition with a ledger rather than a slogan. The core message is that the always-on world is not simply “good” or “bad”: it delivers real, large benefits and imposes real, specific costs, and the two land on different, nameable groups.

That ledger discipline is the most valuable thing to carry forward. Whenever you meet a new technology, you can refuse the lazy summary that “it changed everything” and instead ask the sharper questions — who, specifically, benefited; who, specifically, paid; and in which currency, human, ethical, or environmental. The ubiquitous data and connectivity this transition produced also became the raw material for the next wave of computing, which makes understanding its ledger all the more urgent.

Key Takeaways

  • The internet is the underlying network; the World Wide Web is one hypertext application — browsers, hyperlinks, URLs — that runs on top of it, alongside others like email.
  • The Web (HTML, HTTP, URLs, browsers) made the network browsable for non-specialists and drove mass adoption, evolving from read-only pages to user-generated “Web 2.0.”
  • The smartphone, a convergence device on continuous mobile networks, turned computing from a place you go into an ambient, always-on condition woven into daily life.
  • Network effects concentrated communication and information into a few platforms, giving instant global reach while also enabling virality, filter bubbles, and misinformation.
  • Every benefit and every cost of the transition has a distribution — name the specific winners and cost-bearers, and separate human, ethical, and environmental costs, rather than crediting or blaming “society.”

Reflection Questions

  1. Look at the networked devices and services you used today. For one of them, name the specific benefit you received and the specific cost you may be paying — and decide whether you consider that exchange fair.
  2. The lesson argues the same platform machinery that gives instant global reach also spreads misinformation. In your own experience, where have you seen that double edge play out, and what would have to change to keep the reach while reducing the harm?
  3. Apply the who-benefited / who-paid ledger to a networked technology not discussed here — for example, a fitness wearable, a streaming service, or a delivery-robot fleet. Name one human, one ethical, and one environmental cost, each with the specific group that bears it.
