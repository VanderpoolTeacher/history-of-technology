Lesson 12: The Web, Mobile, and Ubiquitous Computing

Module: 6 — The Internet Age · Hours: 3 (180 min)

Introduction

Reach into your pocket. The device there can summon almost any fact ever written, show you a stranger’s face in real time across an ocean, and pay for your coffee — all while quietly noting where you are and what you tapped. Most of the world now lives inside that always-on connection, yet very few people connected to a global network at all until two specific things happened.

First, a way to browse the network made it usable by ordinary people instead of specialists. Then a device that fit in a pocket made that connection something you carried everywhere rather than something you sat down to do. Miss how recent and how engineered this shift was, and the always-on world looks like natural progress instead of a deliberate transformation with clear winners and clear losers.

In this lesson you will first pin down a distinction people constantly get wrong — the difference between the World Wide Web and the internet itself. Then you will trace the rise of the smartphone and ubiquitous, always-on computing, and the platforms and social media that grew on top of it. Finally, you will weigh the whole transition on a ledger: who benefited, who bore the costs, and across which human, ethical, and environmental dimensions.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to:

  1. Distinguish the World Wide Web from the internet, defining the Web as a hypertext information system (browsers, hyperlinks, URLs) running on top of the internet.
  2. Describe the rise of the smartphone and mobile, ubiquitous, “always-on” computing, and explain how it embedded networked devices into everyday life.
  3. Explain how social media and platform services reshaped how people communicate, form communities, and access information.
  4. Evaluate who benefited and who bore the costs of the networked, always-on transition — across human, ethical, and environmental dimensions. (C10)

Topic 1 — The Web Is Not the Internet

Ask most people what the internet is and they will describe browsing pages, clicking links, and visiting sites. That answer quietly confuses two different things, and untangling them is the single most important conceptual move in this lesson — because nearly every later idea depends on getting it right.

Begin with the distinction itself. The internet is the underlying global network — the plumbing of cables, routers, and addresses that moves data between machines, which you can think of as the lower layer that simply delivers packets anywhere. The World Wide Web (WWW) is something different: it is one application that runs on top of the internet — a hypertext information system for publishing and browsing linked documents — sitting alongside other applications such as email, which also run on the same internet but are not the Web. The Web needs the internet to carry its data, but the internet existed and carried other traffic before the Web, and would keep working if the Web vanished. The Web rides on the internet; it is not the same thing.

What the Web does is make information browsable, and it does so with a small set of building blocks. Hypertext is text containing hyperlinks — clickable connections that jump you from one document to another. Pages are written in HTML (HyperText Markup Language), the language that structures a page’s text and links. They are requested and delivered using HTTP (HyperText Transfer Protocol), the rules a browser and a server use to exchange pages. Each page is located by a URL (Uniform Resource Locator), its unique address on the Web. A browser is the program that fetches a page by its URL, renders the HTML, and follows hyperlinks when you click them, and a server is the machine that stores pages and sends them when asked. Together these turned a network only specialists could use into something anyone could click through.

The Web did not stand still. The earliest Web was largely read-only — a small number of authors published pages and most people only read them. It then shifted to what is often called Web 2.0, an interactive, dynamic Web defined by user-generated content, in which ordinary people, not just publishers, create the material: posts, reviews, videos, and profiles. As a concrete illustration of why this drove mass adoption: before browsers, reaching information on the network meant typing arcane commands and knowing exactly where things lived; after browsers, a person who had never programmed anything could open a page, click a blue underlined word, and land somewhere new. That single act — click a link, arrive somewhere — is what brought the general public onto a network that had existed, unbrowsable, for years.

Topic 2 — Computing in Your Pocket

The Web made the network usable, but you still had to be at a desk to reach it. The next shift moved the connection off the desk and into your pocket — and in doing so changed computing from somewhere you went into something that is simply always there. That structural change, not mere convenience, is the point of this topic.

The engine of the shift was the smartphone — a convergence device that combines what used to be many separate machines into one always-connected object: a phone, a camera, a GPS (Global Positioning System) receiver for location, a music player, and a full internet client, all in a pocket-sized package. What convergence did was collapse a drawer’s worth of devices into one and keep it connected continuously. You no longer chose a device for a task; you carried one device that did nearly everything and was online the whole time.

Two supporting developments made that connection continuous rather than occasional. Mobile networks — wireless data networks that follow you as you move — meant connectivity no longer required plugging in at a fixed location. App ecosystems — curated marketplaces of small, single-purpose programs called apps (applications) — meant new capabilities could be added to the device at any time with a tap. Together these turned going online from an event you “sat down” to do into a background condition of daily life. This is ubiquitous computing, also called “always-on” computing — a state in which networked sensors and devices are woven into everyday environments and routines so thoroughly that you stop noticing them: the watch tracking your steps, the doorbell streaming video, the speaker listening for its name.

As a concrete illustration of the structural shift, contrast two mornings. In the desk era, to check a message or look something up you walked to a machine, sat down, connected, did the task, and got up — computing was a place you went. In the ubiquitous era, you glance at a wrist between steps, a sensor logs your run without being asked, and a notification finds you in line at a store — computing is an ambient condition you are never outside of. Nothing about the second morning required you to “use a computer,” yet you were continuously inside a networked system.

Topic 3 — Platforms, Social Media, and Information

Once nearly everyone carried an always-connected device, a new layer grew on top of it: a small number of services through which a huge share of communication, community, and information now flows. This topic describes that shift — what changed about how people connect and learn — and holds off on judging it until the ledger.

The dominant form this took is social media — online platforms where users create and share content and connect with one another, which have become a primary way people communicate and form community. What platforms do is intermediate: rather than people reaching each other directly, much of online life now passes through a handful of services that sit in the middle, host the content, and decide what each person sees. The reason a few services came to dominate is the network effect — the property by which a service becomes more valuable to each user as more people join it, because the people you want to reach are already there. The network effect is self-reinforcing: the biggest platform is the most useful because it is the biggest, which makes it bigger still, which is why online life concentrated into a few platform services rather than spreading evenly across many.

What this did to information is double-edged, and the goal here is to describe both sides plainly. On one side, platforms gave ordinary people instant global reach — anyone could publish to a potential audience of millions at no cost. On the other side, the same machinery produced new problems: virality, in which content spreads explosively whether or not it is true; filter bubbles, in which a person mostly sees views that match their own because the platform shows them what keeps them engaged; and misinformation, false content that spreads as easily as true content — sometimes faster, because it is engineered to provoke. As a concrete illustration, hold both halves in one image: a single post by an unknown person can reach millions of people overnight, which is a genuinely new and democratizing power — and that same mechanism can carry a false claim just as far, just as fast, to just as many people who never see a correction.

Topic 4 — The Ledger: Who Benefited, Who Paid

Now the lesson turns from describing the always-on world to evaluating it. The central discipline of this topic is to refuse the easy summary that “technology changed society,” and instead name the specific groups on each side of a ledger: who gained, who paid, and in what currency — human, ethical, or environmental. (C10)

Begin with the benefits, because they are real and large. The networked, always-on transition gave billions of people access to information and services that were once scarce or gated; new livelihoods and markets, as people built businesses, audiences, and incomes that could not have existed before; and connection across distance, letting families, collaborators, and communities stay in contact regardless of geography. These gains are not evenly distributed, but they are genuine, and an honest ledger names them first.

Against those gains stand costs that fall into two groups. The human and ethical costs include surveillance and the erosion of privacy — the always-on, always-sensing device that delivers service also continuously collects data about you; the attention economy — platforms engineered to maximize engagement, capturing and holding your attention because attention is what they sell; the digital divide — the gap between those who have reliable, affordable connectivity and devices and those who do not, which now compounds other inequalities; and gig / platform labor — precarious, often unprotected work mediated by platforms, where the worker bears the risk and the platform takes a cut. (C10) The environmental costs include e-waste — the mountains of discarded, hard-to-recycle devices produced by short replacement cycles — and the energy and water demands of data centers, the large facilities that power the always-on world and that consume substantial electricity and water for cooling. (C10)

The synthesis that ties this together is that every benefit has a distribution — it lands on specific people, and so does every cost. As a concrete, fully worked ledger row to model the method: consider a free messaging app. Benefit: users gain free, instant communication across distance (winners: the billions of people who could not previously afford long-distance contact). Human/ethical cost: the service is free because user data and attention are the product (cost-bearers: users whose private behavior is monetized; the mechanism is data collected from every message and tap). Environmental cost: delivering and storing all those messages runs on power-hungry data centers (cost-bearers: communities near those facilities and the broader climate; the mechanism is the energy and water footprint). Notice that this row names specific groups on each side and a specific mechanism for each entry — never “society” in general.

Activity — “Audit the Always-On World”

  • Type: Case study (group analysis and evaluation, not recall).
  • Time: 40 minutes.
  • Description: Your group will take one everyday networked service and put it on trial as a consequences auditor. You will build a two-column ledger — who benefited and who bore the costs — with at least one human, one ethical, and one environmental entry, each tied to a specific mechanism. Then you will rank which cost you judge most consequential and defend that choice to the room.

Instructions:

  1. (3 min) In groups of three or four, take one assigned service. Options:
    • A free social media app (you pay nothing; the service earns from advertising).
    • A ride-hailing platform (an app connects riders with drivers for paid trips).
    • A smart-home device (a voice speaker or video doorbell connected to a network).
  2. (15 min) Build a two-column ledger headed Who Benefited and Who Bore the Costs. Fill in at least: one benefit (with the specific group that gains), and three costs — one human, one ethical, and one environmental — each naming the specific group that pays and the mechanism (what data is collected, what labor is used, what device or energy footprint is involved). Write the mechanism explicitly for every entry.
  3. (10 min) Rank your three costs from most to least consequential. Write one or two sentences defending your top-ranked cost: why is it the most serious, and for whom?
  4. (12 min) Each group presents its service, its ledger, and its top-ranked cost in 90 seconds. The rest of the room listens for which groups keep appearing as winners and which keep appearing as cost-bearers.

Wrap-Up and Reflection

You arrived treating “the internet” and “being online” as one undifferentiated thing; you leave able to separate the network from the Web that rides on it, to name the shift from computing-as-a- place to computing-as-an-ambient-condition, and — most importantly — to evaluate that whole transition with a ledger rather than a slogan. The core message is that the always-on world is not simply “good” or “bad”: it delivers real, large benefits and imposes real, specific costs, and the two land on different, nameable groups.

That ledger discipline is the most valuable thing to carry forward. Whenever you meet a new technology, you can refuse the lazy summary that “it changed everything” and instead ask the sharper questions — who, specifically, benefited; who, specifically, paid; and in which currency, human, ethical, or environmental. The ubiquitous data and connectivity this transition produced also became the raw material for the next wave of computing, which makes understanding its ledger all the more urgent.

Key Takeaways

  • The internet is the underlying network; the World Wide Web is one hypertext application — browsers, hyperlinks, URLs — that runs on top of it, alongside others like email.
  • The Web (HTML, HTTP, URLs, browsers) made the network browsable for non-specialists and drove mass adoption, evolving from read-only pages to user-generated “Web 2.0.”
  • The smartphone, a convergence device on continuous mobile networks, turned computing from a place you go into an ambient, always-on condition woven into daily life.
  • Network effects concentrated communication and information into a few platforms, giving instant global reach while also enabling virality, filter bubbles, and misinformation.
  • Every benefit and every cost of the transition has a distribution — name the specific winners and cost-bearers, and separate human, ethical, and environmental costs, rather than crediting or blaming “society.”

Reflection Questions

  1. Look at the networked devices and services you used today. For one of them, name the specific benefit you received and the specific cost you may be paying — and decide whether you consider that exchange fair.
  2. The lesson argues the same platform machinery that gives instant global reach also spreads misinformation. In your own experience, where have you seen that double edge play out, and what would have to change to keep the reach while reducing the harm?
  3. Apply the who-benefited / who-paid ledger to a networked technology not discussed here — for example, a fitness wearable, a streaming service, or a delivery-robot fleet. Name one human, one ethical, and one environmental cost, each with the specific group that bears it.
