The History of Technology

Three hundred years ago, almost nobody had ever moved faster than a galloping horse, sent a message quicker than a rider could carry it, or owned more than a handful of manufactured things. Today you can cross an ocean before lunch, speak to someone on the far side of the planet in real time, and carry in your pocket a machine more powerful than the ones that first sent people to the Moon. This course is the story of how that happened — and of what it cost.

It is tempting to tell that story as a parade of clever inventions: the steam engine, the light bulb, the automobile, the computer, the smartphone. But inventions do not fall from the sky, and they do not act on the world by themselves. A technology is taken up — or ignored, or fought over — by real societies with their own economies, politics, and values, and in turn it reshapes the daily lives of the people who live with it. That two-way street between technology and society is the thread you will follow from the first chapter to the last.

You will begin around 1750, in a world still running on muscle, water, and wind, and trace the great accelerations that followed: steam and the factory, steel and electricity, the assembly line and the technologies of war, the computer, the internet, and the artificial intelligence of the present day. Along the way you will keep asking the historian’s questions — who benefited, who paid, and why this and not that — rather than simply cataloguing what came when.

By the end, you will not just know the milestones. You will be able to take a single technology of your own choosing, research its origins and consequences, and tell its story with evidence — in writing and out loud. Think of this introduction as the trailhead. The path runs from a quiet pre-industrial village all the way to the machine in your hand right now.
