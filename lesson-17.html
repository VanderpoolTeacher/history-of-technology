Lesson 17: Presenting Your Analysis

Module: 8 — Case-Study Capstone · Hours: 2 (120 min)

Introduction

You have a written case study you are proud of — an arguable thesis about your technology, defended with evidence and analysis. Now you have to stand up and make an audience see that argument in about ten minutes. A strong paper can still fall completely flat when spoken aloud, and the reason is simple: reading and listening are different acts.

A reader can pause, reread, and skim back. A listener gets one pass, in real time, with no rewind. If you present by reading your paper aloud, your audience will be lost by the second paragraph. The skill of presenting is the skill of redesigning your argument for the ear and the eye — selecting, compressing, and supporting it so a listener can follow it the first time.

In this lesson you will structure your analysis as a spoken arc, design slides that reinforce your argument instead of burying it, put a piece of evidence cleanly on a slide, and deliver and field questions with composure. Everything is built for either an in-person room or a recorded/synchronous-online setting, so the same preparation serves whichever mode you present in.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to:

  1. Structure an oral presentation of a technology analysis into a clear arc — hook, thesis, evidence, and conclusion — sized to a set time limit.
  2. Design visual support that reinforces the argument and presents evidence on a slide without overwhelming the audience.
  3. Deliver the presentation persuasively and respond to audience questions, adapting to in-person or recorded/synchronous-online delivery.

Topic 1 — Structuring the Talk

Imagine two presenters with identical research. The first reads three pages of their paper aloud; two minutes in, the audience has stopped following. The second opens with a startling fact, states one clear claim, walks through three pieces of evidence, and lands a conclusion — and the audience tracks every step. Same content, opposite outcome. The difference is structure built for the ear.

The first principle is that a presentation is not the paper read aloud. A spoken talk requires you to select (cut everything but the load-bearing points), sequence (order them so each follows naturally), and compress (say each in the fewest clear words). A paper can carry a dozen sub-arguments; a ten-minute talk carries one thesis and a few beats of support.

The structure that does this is the presentation arc — a fixed sequence that a listener can follow without a map:

  • Hook — a vivid fact, image, or question that earns the audience’s attention in the first fifteen seconds.
  • Thesis — your one arguable claim, stated plainly and early so the audience knows what you contend.
  • Two or three evidence beats — your strongest supporting points, one at a time, each with a piece of evidence.
  • Consequence/impact — the analytical payoff: who benefited, who bore the costs, and how the technology and its society shaped each other.
  • Conclusion — a short restatement of the thesis and why it matters, leaving one idea in the audience’s mind.

Carry your written argument into this arc deliberately. Your thesis becomes the talk’s thesis; your impact analysis — both the two-way relationship between the technology and its society and the accounting of who gained and who paid — becomes the consequence beat. Do not re-research; re-shape what you already proved on the page.

Finally, timebox. A time limit is a hard constraint, and content must be cut to fit it, not crammed. A rough budget for a ten-minute talk: about one minute for the hook and thesis, roughly two minutes per evidence beat, two minutes for consequence, and one minute to conclude. If a point will not fit, cut it — an audience remembers a clear three-beat talk far better than a rushed seven-beat one.

Here is the contrast made concrete. A weak open: “Today I’ll talk about the safety bicycle. It was invented in the 1880s. There’s a lot of background, so let me start with the history of cycling…” The audience is already drifting. A strong open: “In 1885, riding a bicycle could kill you. Ten years later, it was the freedom machine of a generation of women. My argument is that the safety bicycle’s spread was driven less by mechanical novelty than by the risk it removed.” Hook, then thesis, in under twenty seconds — and the audience is leaning in.

Topic 2 — Visual Support and Evidence on a Slide

Picture a slide crammed with eleven bullet points in small type while the presenter reads them aloud. The audience faces an impossible choice: read the slide or listen to the speaker, and they cannot do both. That wall-of-text slide is the single most common way good arguments get buried. This topic is about slides that help instead.

The governing principle is that slides reinforce; they do not transcribe. A slide is visual support for your spoken argument, not a teleprompter. The discipline that follows is one idea per slide with minimal text — a slide should make a single point land faster than your words alone could, then get out of the way.

Slides are also where you put evidence in front of the audience, which is far more persuasive than describing it. Strong evidence slides include:

  • A source image — a period photograph, a patent drawing, an advertisement — that lets the audience see your evidence.
  • A chart — a simple graph of a trend (adoption over time, output, prices) that makes a number visible at a glance.
  • A short attributed quotation — a single sentence from a source, not a paragraph, large enough to read.

Whatever you put up, attribute and caption it. The honesty you practiced while taking notes and writing carries straight onto the screen: every image, chart, and quotation needs a visible source credit, usually a small caption in a corner. An uncredited image on a slide is the visual version of an uncited fact in a paper.

A few design basics keep slides legible: high contrast between text and background (dark text on light, or the reverse), type large enough to read from the back of the room or on a small screen, and generous empty space. When in doubt, remove text.

Here is the contrast made concrete, for the same point — that cycling spread rapidly in the 1890s:

  • Cluttered slide: a title, eight bullet points of full sentences about manufacturing, prices, social debates, and statistics, plus an uncredited photo — too much to read while listening.
  • Clean slide: one short headline (“A craze, not a fad”), one line chart showing UK cycle production climbing through the 1890s, and a small caption reading “Source: Smith, The Bicycle in Victorian Britain, 2015, p. 91.” One idea, one piece of evidence, attributed — the audience gets it in three seconds and returns its attention to you.

Topic 3 — Delivery and Fielding Questions

You can have a perfect arc and beautiful slides and still lose the room if you mumble at your shoes or freeze when someone raises a hand. Delivery is the part students dread most and rehearse least — which is exactly backwards, because it is the most improvable.

Start with delivery fundamentals — the controllable behaviors that make you clear and credible:

  • Pace. Nerves speed you up; slow down deliberately and pause at the end of key points. Silence reads as confidence.
  • Voice. Project, and vary your tone so the important sentences sound important.
  • Eye contact (or camera contact). In a room, meet listeners’ eyes across the audience; on camera, look into the lens, not at your own image, so viewers feel addressed.
  • Managing nerves. Nerves are normal and largely invisible to the audience. Channel them with preparation, a slow first breath, and a memorized first line so the opening runs on rails.

Because this work serves either an in-person or a recorded/synchronous-online presentation, adapt to your mode:

  • In person: use room presence — stand where you can be seen, move with purpose, and read the audience’s faces to adjust. Q&A is a live exchange; take the question, then address the whole room with your answer.
  • Online or recorded: mind the framing (centered, well-lit, camera near eye level), the audio (a close, clear microphone matters more than video quality), and the screen-share (rehearse sharing slides so it is seamless). For Q&A, watch the chat and the unmute, restate each question aloud for everyone, and slow down slightly to absorb the lag.

Then there is the moment students fear: handling questions. Use a four-step move every time: listen to the whole question without interrupting; restate it briefly (this buys you a thinking moment and confirms you understood); answer with evidence drawn from your research; and concede honestly when unsure — “I didn’t find evidence on that, but here’s how I’d look into it” is a strong answer, not a weak one. Bluffing is the only real failure.

A worked example. Question: “Wasn’t the safety bicycle just for the rich, given the price?” A strong response: (restate) “So, was cycling really accessible beyond the wealthy?” (answer with evidence) “Early on, yes, prices were high — but used-cycle markets and installment plans brought costs down through the 1890s, which is part of why adoption widened (Smith, p. 118).” (honest where needed) “I focused on Britain, so I can’t speak to how that played out elsewhere.” Calm, sourced, honest — that is what persuades.

Finally, the path to all of this is rehearsal. Practice the talk aloud, on the clock, at least twice — ideally once in your actual mode (standing in a room, or sharing your screen on camera). Rehearsal is what converts a shaky read into a confident, timed, persuasive delivery; it is not optional polish but the core of preparation.

Activity — “Build and Deliver One Slide”

  • Type: Individual exercise plus paired delivery on the learner’s own capstone project (application).
  • Time: 20 minutes.
  • Description: You will create one evidence slide for your case study — a visual plus an attributed piece of evidence — and then deliver a 60-to-90-second spoken segment of your argument using that slide to a partner, in whichever mode (in person or on camera) you will actually present in. You leave with a tested slide and a rehearsed segment of your talk.

Instructions:

  1. (7 min) Build one evidence slide for your case study. It must contain a single idea, a visual (a source image, a chart, or one short quotation), minimal text, and a visible attribution/caption.
  2. (5 min) Plan a 60-to-90-second spoken segment that uses the slide: state the claim the slide supports, point to the evidence, and interpret what it means for your thesis. Note which mode you will present in (in person or on camera).
  3. (5 min) Deliver the segment to your partner in that mode — standing and facing them, or sharing your screen on camera. Your partner times you and notes one strength and one fix for each of structure, slide, and delivery.
  4. (3 min) Swap roles, then trade your feedback.

Wrap-Up and Reflection

You came in with an argument on the page and leave able to put it in front of an audience. The core message is that presenting is not reciting — it is redesigning your analysis for a listener who gets one pass. A spoken arc makes the argument followable, slides that reinforce and attribute make the evidence land, and rehearsed, mode-aware delivery makes you persuasive and steady, even under questions.

This is the final skill that turns research and writing into communication. A technology analysis that no one can follow when spoken has not yet done its job; one that an audience grasps, believes, and remembers has. The same thesis, the same evidence, and the same honest attribution that carried your written case study now carry your voice.

Key Takeaways

  • A presentation is a redesign of your argument for the ear, not the paper read aloud — select, sequence, and compress.
  • The spoken arc — hook, thesis, evidence beats, consequence, conclusion — lets a listener follow the argument in one pass, sized to the time limit.
  • Slides reinforce rather than transcribe: one idea per slide, evidence shown visually, and every visual attributed.
  • Delivery is improvable through controllable fundamentals — pace, voice, eye/camera contact, managed nerves — and adapts to in-person or online mode.
  • Questions are handled by listening, restating, answering with evidence, and conceding honestly when unsure; rehearsal is the path to confidence.

Reflection Questions

  1. Recall a presentation you sat through that lost you. Using the spoken arc and the slide principles here, diagnose what went wrong and name the one change that would have fixed it most.
  2. Take one paragraph of your written case study and rewrite its opening as a fifteen-second spoken hook plus a one-sentence thesis. What did you have to cut?
  3. Suppose you must present your analysis in the mode you are least comfortable with — on camera if you prefer a room, or in a room if you prefer recording. Name the two adaptations you would prepare first, and why.
